Perspective for decisions that take time.

Practical observations on selling, acquiring, valuing, and preparing a building services company, drawn from years in the industry and at the transaction table.

Featured perspective

To sell, or not to sell?

An owner’s answer should reflect personal readiness, company readiness, market reality, and a credible plan for what follows, not a reaction to a single difficult season.

Every business will eventually change ownership or cease to exist. Thoughtful owners prepare for that reality before circumstances make the decision for them.

For many entrepreneur-founders, the company is both their largest asset and one of the defining experiences of their life. Selling it is not a benign financial event. It requires serious thought about the business, the shareholder, the people who depend on the company, and the person the owner expects to become after the transition.

Do not mistake a difficult season for a durable decision

Owners ask whether to sell when times are good and when the walls seem to be pressing in. The first question is whether the time is personally right or whether the desire to exit is a response to short-term circumstances that can be improved through perseverance and strategic thought.

Operating a service business can feel like running on a treadmill: the owner may believe that stepping away will cause the company to falter. If that is true, the dependence itself should be addressed before a sale. A buyer will see the same risk, and the owner will be negotiating under pressure rather than from a position of readiness.

Personal preference should also align with the company’s results. Recent growth and performance, current performance, and a credible outlook all influence the options available. An owner does not need a perfect company, but should understand which conditions are temporary, which are structural, and which can be strengthened with time.

Have a plan for the future: it begins tomorrow

Exit planning does not require a fixed sale date. It begins with a basic outline and becomes more specific as circumstances change. Goals should be revisited periodically so that the owner, management team, financial reporting, customer relationships, and personal plans are not forced into alignment only after a buyer appears.

Owners can learn by observing other transactions, but they should be circumspect. What appears to be fact from the outside may be incomplete, and what was true for one company may not be true for another. Each transaction has familiar elements, yet each is unique in its markets, people, operating model, risks, and shareholder objectives.

Specialists can contribute knowledge an owner is unlikely to possess alone. Even so, the shareholder must understand and agree with the process and the projected result. Advice is most useful when it improves the owner’s judgment rather than replacing it.

Select the buyer as carefully as the buyer selects the company

Once the plan is made and the owner is ready, attention turns to likely and qualified buyers. Whether the search is managed through an intermediary or by the shareholder, selection matters. Price is important, but so are financial capacity, reputation, operating ability, strategic fit, transition expectations, and the likelihood that the buyer can complete what it proposes.

Certain company characteristics may affect the outcome even when they are not quickly changed. In building services, market area, customer sectors, contract structure, concentration, and the production labor model, whether self-perform or contract management, can materially influence buyer interest and perceived risk. The owner should understand those characteristics and be realistic about their effect.

Plan for the person who emerges after the sale

Selling a company requires an open mind and a willingness to listen to people with relevant expertise. No participant is all-knowing, including the shareholder. At the same time, the owner should remain introspective and prepare for the personal transition as deliberately as the business transition.

A sale does not have to be an ending. It can be the beginning of a new venture, a new role, or a new way of using the experience accumulated while building the company. That opportunity is easier to recognize when the owner has planned for it before closing day.
