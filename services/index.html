For business owners considering a sale

Define the exit before entering the market.

Selling a building services company is rarely a single financial decision. It is the transfer of an organization an owner may have spent decades building, and it can affect family, employees, customers, identity, and future plans.

GPA begins by helping an owner examine the available options and define an exit strategy that fits the business and the shareholder. That includes discussing timing, personal readiness, desired involvement after closing, company performance, management depth, market area, service mix, customer concentration, labor model, and the improvements that may strengthen a future process.

When the time is right to proceed, GPA helps organize the sale effort, prepare information for qualified parties, identify and approach likely buyers, manage confidential communication, support negotiations, and coordinate the business issues that arise through diligence and closing. The objective is not simply to locate a buyer. It is to locate a buyer with the resources, industry fit, and intentions required for a sound transaction.

Potential acquirers may include established building service contractors seeking a strategic expansion, private or public investment groups seeking an industry platform, or qualified first-time buyers seeking an entry point. Each buyer category evaluates risk and opportunity differently. GPA helps the owner understand those distinctions before committing to a path.

Preparation can begin years before a sale.

Exit planning may include strengthening financial reporting, reducing concentration, developing managers, clarifying contracts, aligning operational resources, and establishing realistic expectations about value and transition.

For strategic buyers and acquirers

An acquisition must work beyond the spreadsheet.

A building services acquisition can create immediate scale, new geography, customer relationships, management talent, or entry into a market segment. Those benefits depend on understanding how the target actually operates.

GPA works with strategic buyers already active in the industry, financial buyers evaluating a platform or add-on investment, and qualified individuals seeking to enter the sector. The first step is to define criteria clearly: market area, revenue range, service mix, customer sectors, production model, management requirements, available capital, and the buyer’s capacity to integrate.

During evaluation, GPA helps bring attention to the operating issues that can materially affect fit and value. Recurring revenue is important, but so are contract terms, renewal patterns, customer concentration, labor availability, subcontractor relationships, field supervision, account profitability, quality control, insurance, working capital, and the transferability of customer and employee relationships.

The buyer ultimately remains responsible for independent legal, financial, tax, operational, and environmental diligence. GPA’s role is to add building services context, help organize questions, maintain disciplined communication, and keep transaction structure connected to the realities of the business being acquired.

Valuation and market positioning

Value is a range informed by facts, risk, and buyer perspective.

Over time, GPA developed the ability to assist with valuation-related work ranging from informal market assessments to more detailed analyses appropriate to a defined business purpose.

An informal or “desktop” assessment may help an owner understand a likely market range, identify the factors that support or constrain value, and decide whether further preparation is worthwhile. More detailed work may be appropriate when a client needs a documented analysis for transaction planning, internal decision-making, fairness considerations, estate planning, or another defined purpose.

Revenue and earnings alone do not explain the market position of a building service contractor. Buyers may also weigh the durability of customer contracts, quality of financial records, customer and market concentration, service sectors, growth history, management depth, labor model, capital needs, and the degree to which results depend on the owner personally.

Valuation scope and intended use are established before work begins. Formal appraisal, accounting, tax, estate, and legal conclusions should be coordinated with professionals qualified in those fields. When valuation work supports a sale, GPA can help the wider advisory team evaluate how structure, timing, and transition terms affect the practical result to the owner.

Advisory and consulting services

Some of the most valuable work happens outside a transaction.

GPA’s managing associate and other professionals have served the industry as owners, operators, financial officers, and transaction advisors. That background can be applied to a focused business project even when no immediate sale or acquisition is planned.

Assignments may involve long-term strategic planning, preparation for an eventual exit, operational realignment, financial-resource planning, business restructuring, marketing direction, or the identification and recruitment of professional managers. The exact scope depends on the owner’s priorities and the contribution GPA is qualified to make.

Entrepreneur-led companies often encounter issues that are difficult to evaluate from inside the daily operating cycle. An outside industry perspective can help distinguish a temporary problem from a structural one, identify which changes are realistically achievable, and establish an order of work that does not unnecessarily disrupt customers, employees, or cash flow.

The objective is to improve the owner’s decision-making and, where possible, strengthen the durability and value of the company. GPA does not treat consulting as a collection of generic recommendations. Advice is tied to the facts of the company, the realities of the building services market, and the owner’s ability to execute.

Why industry-specific experience matters

The same numbers can tell different stories in different businesses.

Building services companies share many characteristics with other service businesses, but the details that shape risk and value are distinctly industry-specific.

Contract structure, labor intensity, account supervision, customer retention, service frequency, supply and equipment requirements, geography, union exposure, subcontracting, and the owner’s role all influence how a company should be evaluated. A buyer unfamiliar with the sector may misread these details; an owner may also underestimate which facts matter most to a qualified acquirer.

GPA’s long participation in the industry helps the firm understand the language, operating patterns, buyer landscape, and human concerns behind a transaction. Experience does not eliminate uncertainty or replace independent diligence. It helps the parties ask better questions, recognize issues earlier, and avoid forcing a building services company into a generic transaction formula.
